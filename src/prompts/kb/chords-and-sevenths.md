## Triads
A triad is a three-note chord built by stacking two thirds. From the root up:
- Major triad: root, major 3rd, perfect 5th (C E G). Bright, stable.
- Minor triad: root, minor 3rd, perfect 5th (C Eb G). Darker.
- Diminished triad: root, minor 3rd, diminished 5th (C Eb Gb). Tense, unstable.
- Augmented triad: root, major 3rd, augmented 5th (C E G#). Dreamy, unsettled.
The quality of the 3rd (major vs minor) sets the basic color; the 5th distinguishes diminished and augmented.

## Inversions
A chord is in root position when its root is the lowest note. Putting a different chord tone in the bass gives an inversion: first inversion has the 3rd in the bass (C/E), second inversion has the 5th in the bass (C/G). Inversions keep the same chord identity but change the bass line, letting harmonies connect smoothly. A seventh chord also has a third inversion, with the 7th in the bass.

## Figured bass
Figured bass is a shorthand from the Baroque era: numbers below a bass note show the intervals above it. For triads, root position is 5/3 (usually unmarked), first inversion is 6 (often written as a "6 chord"), and second inversion is 6/4. For seventh chords: root position 7, first inversion 6/5, second inversion 4/3, third inversion 4/2 (or just 2). Roman-numeral analysis borrows these figures, so V6/5 means a dominant seventh in first inversion.

## Seventh chords
Adding a third above the triad's 5th gives a seventh chord, named by the triad plus the kind of 7th:
- Dominant 7th (e.g. G7): major triad + minor 7th. Restless; wants to resolve to the tonic.
- Major 7th (Cmaj7): major triad + major 7th. Lush, calm.
- Minor 7th (Dm7): minor triad + minor 7th. Smooth, mellow.
- Minor-major 7th (CmM7): minor triad + major 7th. Tense, cinematic.
- Augmented 7th and others are rarer variants.

## Diminished and half-diminished sevenths
- Half-diminished 7th (written m7b5 or ø7, e.g. Bm7b5): diminished triad + minor 7th. The ii chord of a minor key; leads toward the dominant.
- Fully diminished 7th (dim7 or °7, e.g. B°7): diminished triad + diminished 7th. Built of stacked minor thirds, symmetrical, and very tense — a common pivot for modulation because any of its notes can act as the root.

## Extended chords (9, 11, 13)
Stacking further thirds above a seventh chord gives extensions. A 9th adds the note a 9th above the root (a 2nd, up an octave); an 11th adds the 4th; a 13th adds the 6th. So C9 is a dominant 7th plus a 9th, Cmaj9 is a major 7th plus a 9th, Dm11 stacks up to the 11th. Extensions add richness and are central to jazz; in practice players omit notes (often the 5th, sometimes the root) to keep the chord playable.

## Suspended and added-tone chords
A suspended chord replaces the 3rd with a neighbor: sus4 uses the 4th (Csus4 = C F G), sus2 uses the 2nd (Csus2 = C D G). With no 3rd, they sound open and unresolved, often resolving to the plain triad. An added-tone chord keeps the 3rd and adds a note without the intervening thirds: Cadd9 is a triad plus the 9th, with no 7th (unlike C9).

## Slash chords
A slash chord names the chord on the left and the bass note on the right: C/G means a C major triad played over a G in the bass. The bass note may be a chord tone (an inversion, like C/E) or a non-chord tone for a specific bass line. Slash chords are common in pop and folk lead sheets and chord charts.

## Reading chord symbols
In lead-sheet and chord-chart notation: a bare letter is major (C); "m" or "-" is minor (Cm); "dim" or "°" diminished; "aug" or "+" augmented; "7" a dominant 7th; "maj7" or "Δ" a major 7th; "m7b5" or "ø" half-diminished; numbers like 9/11/13 mean extensions; "sus2"/"sus4" suspensions; "add9" an added tone. The root letter can carry an accidental (Bb, F#), and a slash adds a bass note (Am7/G).
