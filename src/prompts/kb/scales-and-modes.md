## The major scale
A major scale follows the step pattern whole-whole-half-whole-whole-whole-half (W W H W W W H). Starting on C gives all white keys: C D E F G A B C. The half steps fall between the 3rd–4th and 7th–8th degrees. This pattern is the reference every other scale and mode is measured against.

## Scale degree names
Each degree of a major or minor scale has a function name: 1 tonic, 2 supertonic, 3 mediant, 4 subdominant, 5 dominant, 6 submediant, 7 leading tone (when a semitone below the tonic) or subtonic (when a whole tone below, as in natural minor). The tonic is home; the dominant (5) creates the strongest pull back to it; the leading tone (7) "leans" up into the tonic.

## Natural minor scale
The natural minor scale follows W H W W H W W. A natural minor is all white keys: A B C D E F G A. Compared with the major scale of the same starting note (parallel major), natural minor has a lowered 3rd, 6th, and 7th (b3, b6, b7), which gives it its darker sound.

## Harmonic and melodic minor
Natural minor lacks a leading tone, weakening the pull to the tonic, so two variants raise the 7th. Harmonic minor raises the 7th by a semitone (A B C D E F G# A), creating a strong leading tone but an awkward augmented-2nd gap between the 6th and 7th. Melodic minor raises both the 6th and 7th ascending (A B C D E F# G# A) to smooth that gap, and traditionally reverts to natural minor descending.

## Relative major and minor
Every major key shares its key signature with a minor key a minor third below — its relative minor, built on the 6th degree of the major. C major and A minor both have no sharps or flats; G major and E minor both have one sharp. They use the same notes but treat a different note as home.

## Parallel major and minor
Parallel keys share the same tonic but differ in quality: C major and C minor. Switching between them (borrowing chords or shifting mode) is a common expressive device. Unlike relatives, parallels have different key signatures (C major 0 accidentals, C minor 3 flats).

## The modes
The seven modes are the major scale started on each of its seven degrees, each with a distinct character:
- Ionian (1st degree) = the major scale.
- Dorian (2nd) = minor with a raised 6th; jazzy, folk.
- Phrygian (3rd) = minor with a lowered 2nd; Spanish, dark.
- Lydian (4th) = major with a raised 4th; bright, dreamy.
- Mixolydian (5th) = major with a lowered 7th; bluesy, rock.
- Aeolian (6th) = the natural minor scale.
- Locrian (7th) = diminished, with a lowered 2nd and 5th; unstable, rare.
The white-key modes are C Ionian, D Dorian, E Phrygian, F Lydian, G Mixolydian, A Aeolian, B Locrian.

## Pentatonic scales
A pentatonic scale has five notes per octave. The major pentatonic is the major scale without its 4th and 7th (C D E G A) — no semitones, so nothing clashes, which makes it forgiving for improvising. The minor pentatonic is the natural minor without its 2nd and 6th (A C D E G); it is the backbone of rock, blues, and pop soloing.

## The blues scale
The blues scale is the minor pentatonic plus an added chromatic "blue note," the flat 5th: A C D Eb E G. That extra note, used as a passing tone, gives blues, rock, and jazz their characteristic bent, expressive sound.

## Chromatic and whole-tone scales
The chromatic scale uses all twelve semitones in order and has no tonic of its own; it supplies passing notes and tension. The whole-tone scale divides the octave into six equal whole steps (C D E F# G# A#), sounds ambiguous and "floating" because no note feels like home, and is associated with Impressionist composers like Debussy.
