## Notes and the chromatic scale
Western music uses twelve pitches per octave, called the chromatic scale: C, C#/Db, D, D#/Eb, E, F, F#/Gb, G, G#/Ab, A, A#/Bb, B, then C again an octave higher. The seven "natural" letter names (A–G) are the white keys on a piano; the five sharps/flats between them are the black keys. There is no black key between E–F and B–C, so those pairs are only a semitone apart.

## Semitones, tones, and octaves
A semitone (half step) is the smallest distance in standard Western music — one key to the very next key on a piano, black or white. A whole tone (whole step) is two semitones. An octave is twelve semitones; two notes an octave apart share the same letter name and sound "the same but higher/lower" because one vibrates at exactly twice the frequency of the other.

## Accidentals and enharmonics
A sharp (#) raises a note by a semitone; a flat (b) lowers it by a semitone; a natural (♮) cancels a previous sharp or flat. A double sharp (x) raises by two semitones, a double flat (bb) lowers by two. Two names for the same pitch are called enharmonic equivalents — for example C# and Db are the same key, as are F# and Gb. Which spelling you use depends on the key and the harmony.

## Intervals
An interval is the distance between two pitches, named by a number (how many letter names it spans) and a quality. Counting is inclusive: C up to E spans C-D-E = a third. Qualities are perfect (P), major (M), minor (m), augmented (A), and diminished (d). Unisons, fourths, fifths, and octaves can be perfect; seconds, thirds, sixths, and sevenths can be major or minor.

## Interval sizes in semitones
Unison 0, minor 2nd 1, major 2nd 2, minor 3rd 3, major 3rd 4, perfect 4th 5, tritone (augmented 4th / diminished 5th) 6, perfect 5th 7, minor 6th 8, major 6th 9, minor 7th 10, major 7th 11, octave 12. A major interval made one semitone smaller becomes minor; a perfect or major interval made one semitone larger becomes augmented; a perfect or minor interval made one semitone smaller becomes diminished.

## Consonance and dissonance
Consonant intervals sound stable and restful: unisons, octaves, perfect fifths and fourths (perfect consonances), and major/minor thirds and sixths (imperfect consonances). Dissonant intervals sound tense and want to resolve: major/minor seconds and sevenths, and the tritone. Dissonance is not "bad" — it creates the motion and release that drives music forward.

## The staff, clefs, and middle C
Music is written on a staff of five lines and four spaces. A clef fixes which pitches the lines and spaces represent. The treble clef (G clef) is used for higher instruments and the right hand at the piano; its lines from bottom to top are E-G-B-D-F and its spaces spell F-A-C-E. The bass clef (F clef) is for lower pitches and the left hand; its lines are G-B-D-F-A and its spaces A-C-E-G. Middle C sits on a ledger line just below the treble staff and just above the bass staff, linking the two in the grand staff used for piano.

## Ledger lines
When a note is too high or low for the staff, short ledger lines extend the staff to hold it. Middle C on a single treble staff sits on the first ledger line below; the C two octaves above middle C sits on ledger lines above the treble staff.

## Key signatures
A key signature is the group of sharps or flats written at the start of each line, applying to every octave of those notes throughout the piece. It tells you the key. Sharps always appear in the order F, C, G, D, A, E, B (mnemonic "Father Charles Goes Down And Ends Battle"); flats appear in the reverse order, B, E, A, D, G, C, F.

## The circle of fifths
The circle of fifths arranges keys by ascending perfect fifths, each adding one sharp: C (0), G (1#), D (2#), A (3#), E (4#), B (5#), F# (6#). Going the other way by fifths (descending, or ascending fourths) adds flats: C (0), F (1b), Bb (2b), Eb (3b), Ab (4b), Db (5b), Gb (6b). It is the map of how keys relate: neighboring keys differ by only one accidental and sound closely related, which is why music often modulates to the key a fifth away.

## Finding a major key from its signature
For sharp keys, the major key is one semitone above the last sharp: with F#-C#-G# the last sharp is G#, so the key is A major. For flat keys, the major key is the second-to-last flat: with Bb-Eb-Ab the second-to-last is Eb, so the key is Eb major. (F major, one flat, is the exception you simply memorize.)
