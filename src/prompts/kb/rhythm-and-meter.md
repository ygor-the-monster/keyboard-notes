## Beat, meter, and time signatures
The beat is the steady pulse you tap your foot to. Meter groups beats into repeating bars. A time signature shows this with two numbers: the top is how many beats per bar, the bottom is which note value gets one beat (4 = quarter note, 8 = eighth note, 2 = half note). So 4/4 is four quarter-note beats per bar, 3/4 is three.

## Simple vs compound meter
In simple meter each beat divides into two (4/4, 3/4, 2/4). In compound meter each beat divides into three, and the time signature's top number is a multiple of three counting the divisions: 6/8 is two beats of three eighth-notes each, 9/8 is three such beats, 12/8 is four. So 6/8 feels like two big "dotted-quarter" beats, not six even ones. Common signatures: 4/4 (common time), 3/4 (waltz), 2/4 (march), 6/8 (compound duple), 12/8 (slow blues/ballad).

## Note durations
Each note value is half the length of the one before it: whole note (4 beats in 4/4), half note (2), quarter note (1), eighth note (½), sixteenth note (¼), thirty-second (⅛). Rests mirror these durations exactly but mean silence. Beaming joins the flags of eighth and shorter notes into groups that show the beat, making rhythm easier to read.

## Dots and ties
A dot after a note adds half its value again: a dotted half note = half + quarter = 3 beats; a dotted quarter = quarter + eighth = 1½ beats. A second dot adds half of the first dot's value. A tie connects two notes of the same pitch into one sustained sound, used to hold a note across a barline or to make durations a single value can't write.

## Tuplets and triplets
A tuplet squeezes an "irregular" number of notes into the time of a normal grouping. The most common is the triplet: three notes in the time of two of the same value (an eighth-note triplet fills one quarter-note beat with three even notes). Duplets and quadruplets do the reverse in compound meter; quintuplets fit five, and so on.

## Syncopation
Syncopation places accents off the expected strong beats — on weak beats or between beats (the offbeats) — creating a sense of push, groove, or surprise. Ties, rests on strong beats, and accented upbeats all produce syncopation. It is central to jazz, funk, Latin, and most popular music.

## Swing
Swing is a performance feel where pairs of eighth notes are played long-short rather than evenly, roughly as the first two notes of a triplet. It is usually written as plain eighth notes with a "swing" instruction rather than spelled out. Swing gives jazz and blues their characteristic lilt.

## Tempo
Tempo is the speed of the beat, measured in beats per minute (BPM). Traditional Italian terms give approximate ranges: Largo (very slow, ~40–60), Adagio (slow, ~66–76), Andante (walking, ~76–108), Moderato (moderate, ~108–120), Allegro (fast, ~120–168), Presto (very fast, ~168–200). A metronome marking like ♩ = 120 states it exactly: 120 quarter-note beats per minute.
