## Diatonic triads of a major key
Building a triad on each degree of a major scale, using only notes in the key, gives a fixed pattern of qualities, labeled with Roman numerals (uppercase = major, lowercase = minor, ° = diminished): I, ii, iii, IV, V, vi, vii°. In C major: C, Dm, Em, F, G, Am, B°. The uppercase/lowercase tells you the chord quality at a glance.

## Diatonic triads of a minor key
In natural minor the pattern is i, ii°, III, iv, v, VI, VII. In A minor: Am, B°, C, Dm, Em, F, G. In practice the 7th is often raised (harmonic minor) so the dominant becomes major (V) with a leading tone, giving the strong V–i pull; this also turns vii into a diminished vii°.

## Harmonic function
Diatonic chords group into three functions that explain how progressions move:
- Tonic (T): I and vi (and iii) — home, rest.
- Predominant / subdominant (PD): IV and ii — departure, setting up the dominant.
- Dominant (D): V and vii° — maximum tension, pulling back to tonic.
The basic cycle is Tonic → Predominant → Dominant → Tonic. Most common progressions are variations on that journey.

## Cadences
A cadence is a harmonic punctuation mark at the end of a phrase:
- Perfect authentic cadence (PAC): V–I with both chords in root position and the tonic in the top voice. The strongest, most final close.
- Imperfect authentic cadence (IAC): V–I that is weaker (an inversion, or not the tonic on top).
- Half cadence: a phrase ending on V — it sounds unfinished, like a comma, expecting more.
- Plagal cadence: IV–I, the "Amen" cadence; gentle and conclusive.
- Deceptive cadence: V–vi instead of the expected V–I; the resolution is dodged, prolonging the music.

## Common progressions
- I–IV–V(–I): the backbone of countless folk, blues, and rock songs.
- I–V–vi–IV: the "four-chord" pop progression behind a huge number of hits.
- ii–V–I: the fundamental jazz cadence; in C major that is Dm7–G7–Cmaj7.
- I–vi–IV–V: the "50s" doo-wop progression.
- vi–IV–I–V: another hugely popular pop rotation of the same chords.

## The twelve-bar blues
The twelve-bar blues is a 12-measure form built on I, IV, and V (often as dominant 7th chords). A standard layout by bar: I-I-I-I / IV-IV-I-I / V-IV-I-V. The final V (the "turnaround") leads back to the top. It underpins blues, early rock and roll, and much of jazz.

## Secondary dominants
A secondary dominant is a dominant chord borrowed from another key to tonicize (temporarily point at) a chord other than the tonic. It is written V/x — "V of x." For example V/V in C major is D7, which resolves to G (the V), making that arrival feel stronger. Secondary dominants add chromatic color and momentum without leaving the key for long.

## Borrowed chords and modal mixture
Modal mixture (borrowing) takes chords from the parallel key — usually borrowing minor-key chords into a major key. Common examples in major are the minor iv, the bVII, and the bVI. Borrowing darkens or warms a passage while keeping the same tonic, and is everywhere in pop, film, and rock harmony.
